# Region — Contracts & Standard Terms

> **Owner:** The agent populating this folder.
> **What this is:** Local norms for residential leases and sale agreements. The specialist surfaces these for rental listings and sale-prep contexts; the specialist DOES NOT draft contracts.

> **Verification note:** Conventions below describe what is *typical* in the Khao Lak foreign-marketed segment. Every deal is negotiable, and Thai contract law differs in meaningful ways from common-law jurisdictions buyers may be used to. Always defer to a Thai lawyer.

---

## Residential lease — typical terms

- **Term length:**
  - **Short-term holiday lets** (under 30 days per booking): the bulk of Khao Lak rental activity. Governed by hotel/license regulations rather than residential lease law; many condo buildings restrict daily rentals via juristic-person rules.
  - **Mid-term seasonal lets** (1–6 months): the dominant "snowbird" lease shape — November to April for European retirees escaping winter. Usually written contracts but often unregistered.
  - **Long-term residential** (1+ year): smaller segment. 1-year written contracts standard.
  - **Registered long-term lease**: any lease over 3 years must be registered at the Land Office to be enforceable beyond the first 3 years. Maximum registrable term: 30 years.
- **Renewal norms:** For seasonal snowbird lets, informal first-right-of-refusal is the norm — same tenant returning year after year. Written renewal options in long-term leases are common but renewal of registered leases beyond 30 years is not legally guaranteed (see regulations.md).
- **Deposit / security:** Typically 1–2 months' rent for long-term; 1 month or fixed amount for seasonal. Held by landlord, not in escrow (no broad escrow norm in Thai residential leasing). Surface this to foreign tenants — they often expect escrow protection.
- **Indexation / rent escalation:** Not standard in Khao Lak residential. Long-term leases sometimes include a fixed step-up at renewal; CPI-linking is rare.
- **Common contingencies:** Furniture inventory list (essential — standard furnished lets have detailed inventory), pool/garden maintenance allocation, internet provision, aircon servicing schedule.
- **Who pays what:**
  - **Owner pays:** Land and Building Tax, condo common-area fees (juristic-person fees), structural maintenance, building insurance.
  - **Tenant pays:** Electricity, water (often metered), internet (sometimes included in furnished lets), gas, minor consumables.
  - **Negotiable:** Pool service, garden service, aircon cleaning — varies by deal. Spell out explicitly.

## Sale agreement — typical terms

- **Deposit structure:**
  - **Reservation deposit:** ฿100,000–฿500,000 typical at LOI / reservation, refundable subject to specified conditions.
  - **First instalment at contract signing:** Commonly 10%–20% of price.
  - **Balance at transfer / Land Office registration:** Remainder paid on the day of transfer at the Land Office.
  - **For new-build / off-plan:** Staged payments tied to construction milestones (foundation, roof, finishing, handover). Verify milestone definitions and remedies for delay.
- **Common contingencies:**
  - Title search clean at Land Office.
  - Condo: foreign-quota letter from juristic person (if foreign buyer taking freehold).
  - Foreign buyer: receipt of FET (Foreign Exchange Transaction form) for inbound funds.
  - Existing mortgage discharge (if seller has a Thai bank mortgage on the property).
  - Building permit / completion certificate for newer construction.
  - Survey / inspection — less standardized than US/UK; foreign buyers should be encouraged to commission an independent inspection.
- **Closing timeline:** 30–60 days from signed sale-and-purchase agreement to Land Office transfer is typical for resale. Off-plan: per construction schedule, often 12–24 months. Cash deals can close faster; deals with mortgage discharge or foreign-quota verification can run longer.
- **Documents the buyer must provide:**
  - Passport (or Thai ID for Thai buyers).
  - For foreign buyer condo freehold: FET form for inbound funds.
  - For foreign buyer leasehold villa: passport + funds documentation.
  - For company-structure purchase: company affidavit, shareholder list, board resolution authorizing the purchase.
  - Power of attorney (Land Office form) if not present at transfer.
- **Documents the seller must provide:**
  - Original Chanote / title deed.
  - House Registration Book (Tabien Baan / ทะเบียนบ้าน).
  - Mortgage discharge letter from bank (if applicable).
  - Tax payment receipts.
  - For condos: juristic-person certificate of no outstanding common fees + foreign-quota letter.
  - For leasehold transfers: original lease agreement.
  - Marriage certificate / divorce papers if marital property.

## Common gotchas

What buyers and renters in your market often miss. The specialist surfaces these as agent-prep notes.

1. **The "freehold villa" misnomer.** Foreigners cannot own land in Thailand. Almost every "freehold villa" sold to a foreigner is either (a) leasehold land + freehold structure, or (b) Thai company holding the land. Buyers from common-law markets often miss this distinction entirely.
2. **The 30+30+30 lease promise.** Marketed as "90-year lease" but only the first 30 years are reliably enforceable. Renewals are contractual promises, not registrable rights, and a new owner of the underlying land may not honor them.
3. **The FET form.** Foreign condo buyers MUST have the Foreign Exchange Transaction form from their inbound remittance to register freehold at the Land Office, AND need it again to repatriate funds at sale. Lost FET = serious problem at exit.
4. **49% foreign quota.** Condo projects can hit their foreign-ownership cap. A buyer can sign an agreement and discover at registration that the building is over quota — forcing a switch to leasehold or deal collapse. Confirm quota status BEFORE deposit.
5. **Title type confusion.** Not all "title deeds" are equal — Chanote vs. Nor Sor 3 Gor vs. lower grades. Insist on Chanote for any meaningful transaction.
6. **Buyer's brokerage isn't standard.** Thai market typically pays commission from seller side only. Foreign buyers expecting a "buyer's agent" relationship need this clarified upfront.
7. **Lawyer ≠ closing agent.** Thai Land Office transfers are direct between parties (or POAs); the lawyer's role is diligence and document prep, not "closing the deal" in the US sense. Set foreign-buyer expectations.
8. **Tsunami zoning and elevation.** Coastal Khao Lak has post-2004 building restrictions and tsunami evacuation zones. Foreign buyers often want to know — surface this proactively rather than reactively.
