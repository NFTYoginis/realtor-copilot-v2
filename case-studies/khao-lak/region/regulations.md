## Purchase tax / transfer tax

- **Tax name:** Transfer fee (ค่าธรรมเนียมการโอน) plus, depending on holding period and seller type: Specific Business Tax (ภาษีธุรกิจเฉพาะ), Stamp Duty (อากรแสตมป์), and Withholding Tax (ภาษีหัก ณ ที่จ่าย).
- **Brackets / rates for resident buyers:**
  - Transfer fee: 2% of the appraised value (registered at the Land Office). [VERIFY CURRENT — government has periodically reduced this to 0.01% as a stimulus measure; confirm at time of transfer.]
  - Specific Business Tax: 3.3% of sale price or appraised value (whichever higher), if seller has owned <5 years AND is not registered as primary residence.
  - Stamp Duty: 0.5% of sale price — applies only when SBT does NOT apply (the two are mutually exclusive).
  - Withholding Tax: progressive for individual sellers (calculated on appraised value with deductions based on holding period); flat 1% of higher-of-price-or-appraised for company sellers.
  - **Who customarily pays what:** Negotiable but conventionally in Khao Lak resort segment: transfer fee split 50/50 between buyer and seller; SBT/stamp duty + withholding paid by seller. Confirm in every contract.
- **Brackets / rates for non-resident or foreign buyers:** Same rates as residents — Thailand does NOT impose a foreign-buyer surcharge on transfer taxes (unlike e.g., Singapore, Australia, parts of Canada). The cost difference for foreign buyers comes from structure (condo freehold vs. leasehold vs. company) and from FX/remittance compliance, not from tax surcharge.
- **Notable exemptions:** Reduced transfer fees periodically applied to first-home purchases under price thresholds (Thai resident programs only). [VERIFY CURRENT]

## Capital gains tax on disposal

- **Tax name:** Thailand does not have a separate capital gains tax on real-estate disposal for individuals. Gains are captured through the Withholding Tax + Personal Income Tax framework at sale.
- **Standard rules:** For an individual seller, withholding tax is calculated at the Land Office on appraised value, with progressive PIT-style brackets and a deduction schedule based on holding period (more years held = larger deduction). The withheld amount is treated as final tax for most resident individuals.
- **Foreign-seller / non-resident rules:** Same withholding mechanics apply at the Land Office regardless of seller nationality. Practical consideration: foreign sellers must repatriate sale proceeds — Thai banks require documentation linking the original inbound FX (the Foreign Exchange Transaction form, formerly Tor Tor 3) to the outbound transfer. Surfacing point: foreign sellers who didn't keep the FET form from purchase will have a friction point on repatriation.

## Property tax / annual fees

- **Local name:** Land and Building Tax (ภาษีที่ดินและสิ่งปลูกสร้าง) — replaced the older House and Land Tax in 2020.
- **How calculated:** Progressive brackets on appraised value, with rate depending on use class:
  - Residential (owner's primary): lowest bracket, with exemptions under value thresholds.
  - Residential (second home / not primary): mid bracket.
  - Commercial / rental use: higher bracket.
  - Vacant / unused land: highest bracket, escalating every few years if undeveloped.
  - Rates are low by Western standards — typically a small fraction of 1% — but the structure matters for foreign buyers because most are second-home users. [VERIFY CURRENT brackets with lawyer.]
- **Who pays in a rental scenario:** Owner pays the annual Land and Building Tax. Common-area / juristic-person fees (ค่าส่วนกลาง) at condominium projects are typically owner-paid; some long-term rental contracts shift utilities and minor common fees to tenant.

## Ownership registration

- **Title types in this market:** Thai title deeds form a hierarchy — buyers and agents must always confirm which type. From strongest to weakest:
  - **Chanote (โฉนดที่ดิน, Nor Sor 4 Jor / น.ส. 4 จ.):** Full freehold title with GPS-surveyed boundaries. The standard for transactable real estate. **Insist on this for any meaningful transaction.**
  - **Nor Sor 3 Gor (น.ส. 3 ก.):** Confirmed possession, aerial-survey boundaries, can be upgraded to Chanote. Transactable but requires extra diligence.
  - **Nor Sor 3 (น.ส. 3):** Possession right, no precise boundaries. Caution.
  - **Sor Kor 1 (ส.ค. 1) / Por Bor Tor 5 (ภ.บ.ท. 5):** Tax-receipt-level claim only. Not real title. Avoid.
- **What an agent should verify before transfer:**
  1. Title type (Chanote required for foreign-buyer condo or leasehold-villa transactions).
  2. Land Office search for liens, mortgages, court orders.
  3. Servitudes and access rights (especially for inland villa lots — beach access, road access, water).
  4. For condos: confirm the building's foreign-ownership quota has not exceeded 49% of total floor area.
  5. For leasehold villas: confirm the underlying land's title and the lease registration at the Land Office (registered leases >3 years; unregistered leases enforceable only 3 years).
  6. Building permit and "House Registration Book" (ทะเบียนบ้าน, Tabien Baan) — Yellow Book available for foreigners.
  7. Environmental and zoning: coastal setbacks, building-height limits (much of Khao Lak's coastal strip is height-limited; some zones tsunami-restricted).
- **Common gotchas:**
  - Properties marketed as "freehold villas" to foreigners almost always involve a leasehold structure or a Thai company holding the land. Always clarify the actual legal structure to the buyer.
  - Thai company structures used for foreign land ownership where Thai shareholders are nominees are illegal under Thai law. The structure is widely used historically but carries real risk; always defer to a lawyer.
  - "30-year lease + two 30-year renewals = 90 years" is commonly marketed but only the first 30 years are reliably enforceable. Renewals are contractual promises, not registrable rights.

## Foreign-buyer rules

- **Restrictions on foreign ownership:**
  - **Land:** Foreigners CANNOT own land directly under Thai law (Land Code Act). Narrow exceptions exist (BOI investment of ฿40M+, treaty-specific cases) but are rarely relevant for residential buyers.
  - **Condominiums:** Foreigners CAN own condo units in freehold, provided total foreign ownership at the project does not exceed 49% of aggregate floor area, AND the purchase funds were remitted into Thailand from abroad in foreign currency (documented via the Foreign Exchange Transaction form / FET).
  - **Houses / villas (the structure only, not the land):** A foreigner CAN own the house structure separately from the land. Common Khao Lak structure: foreigner owns the house + leases the land 30 years (registered).
  - **Leasehold:** Up to 30 years registrable; renewals are contractual only.
  - **Thai company structure:** Available but legally fragile if Thai shareholders are nominees. Defer entirely to a lawyer.
- **Additional taxes or surcharges:** None on the buy side specifically for foreigners (unlike Singapore ABSD, Vancouver foreign-buyer tax, etc.). Cost differences come from structure, FX compliance, and lawyer fees, not surcharge.
- **Documentation requirements:**
  - Passport.
  - Foreign Exchange Transaction form (FET, formerly Tor Tor 3) for any inbound remittance ≥ USD 50,000 equivalent — bank issues this and it is required at the Land Office for condo freehold registration. **Critical: tell every foreign buyer to keep the FET; it's required again at exit/repatriation.**
  - Source-of-funds for AML purposes — Thai banks have tightened.
  - For condo registration: a letter from the condo's juristic person confirming the building remains under the 49% foreign quota.
  - Marriage certificate if the structure involves a Thai spouse.
- **Power-of-attorney for remote close:** Common and accepted. POA must be on the prescribed Land Office form, signed and (for foreign signatories) notarized + legalized / apostilled depending on country, and translated into Thai. Plan 2–4 weeks for international document chain.
