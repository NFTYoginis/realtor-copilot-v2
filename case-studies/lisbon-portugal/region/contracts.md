# Contracts — Lisbon, Portugal

Surface only — defer to a Portuguese real-estate attorney for specifics. The transaction structure here is Roman-law-derived and notary-mediated; substantially different from common-law markets like Marin County.

## Transaction stages

### 1. Reserva (reservation) — optional

- Buyer signs a reservation agreement and pays a small deposit (often €5K–€20K) to take the property off market while diligence runs.
- Refundable conditions vary by agreement. Common in Lisbon practice but not universal.

### 2. CPCV — Contrato de Promessa de Compra e Venda (promissory contract)

- The binding "we are going to buy and sell" agreement.
- Buyer typically pays **10–30%** of purchase price as *sinal* (earnest money / down payment).
- Specifies price, deadlines, conditions, what happens on default.
- **Default rules are important:** if buyer defaults, seller typically keeps the sinal. If seller defaults, buyer is typically owed double the sinal.
- CPCV is often signed at the lawyer's office, sometimes with a notary.
- Time between CPCV and escritura is typically 30–90 days for the buyer to arrange financing, finalize diligence, etc.

### 3. Escritura (deed signing) — notary stage

- The closing event. Held at a Notary (cartório notarial) — public official, neutral, mediates the transfer.
- Notary verifies identity, capacity, title, encumbrances, tax status (Caderneta + Certidão pulled).
- Buyer pays remaining purchase price (already-paid sinal credited).
- IMT and IS paid before or at the escritura (typically before).
- Notary registers the transfer with Conservatória do Registo Predial.

### 4. Registration

- The notary or the buyer's lawyer completes title registration with Conservatória.
- Registration is what makes the buyer the legal owner of record.

## Diligence checklist (pre-CPCV)

These should be pulled and reviewed before any CPCV signing:

| Item | Where it comes from | What to check |
|---|---|---|
| Caderneta Predial | Finanças | Property identification, tax-value, classification |
| Certidão de Teor (or Certidão Permanente) | Conservatória do Registo Predial | Ownership, encumbrances, mortgages, easements |
| Licença de Utilização | Município (Câmara Municipal) | Permitted use of the property — habitação, comércio, etc. Mismatched-use is a real gotcha. |
| Ficha Técnica de Habitação (for post-2004 buildings) | Construction record | Construction details, materials |
| Certificado Energético | Energy authority | Required at sale |
| Condomínio (if part of a building) | Assembleia de Condóminos minutes | Reserves, planned works, special assessments, disputes |
| AL license status (if applicable) | Turismo de Portugal registry | Active license / containment zone status / transferability |

## Common gotchas

### License / use mismatch

A common failure: the property is being used as residential, but the *Licença de Utilização* says commercial (or vice versa). This blocks bank financing in many cases and requires a regularization process before sale. Pull the license early.

### Heritage / listing constraints

Properties in Chiado, Alfama, Baixa, parts of Bairro Alto, Avenida da Liberdade have heritage classifications. Renovation scope is constrained. Buyers with renovation plans need to verify approval pathways before close.

### AL license status

If a buyer is paying for AL-license-attached value, **verify the license is in good standing, not the building's, and is transferable.** Containment-zone rules limit new issuance, which makes existing licenses valuable, but also subject to scrutiny.

### Condomínio reserves and planned works

Many older central Lisbon buildings have under-reserved condomínios. Buyers face surprise special assessments for façade restoration, elevator replacement, structural work. **Pull recent assembly minutes and reserve fund balance before CPCV.**

### Encumbrances / mortgage on title

Outstanding mortgage on the seller's side is normal but must be discharged at escritura. Less common: outstanding lien from contractor, tax authority, or condomínio. The Certidão should surface these but verify.

### Foreign-buyer specifics

- **NIF** (taxpayer number) and Portuguese bank account required to transact for most paths. Start both early.
- **Source-of-funds** documentation required for AML — bank-statement chain proving the funds' origin. Some nationalities face stricter scrutiny.
- **Power of attorney** common for remote close — drafted by the lawyer, signed at consulate or apostilled.
- **Currency** — purchase is in EUR. Buyers funding from USD/GBP/BRL face exchange-rate exposure between CPCV and escritura (30–90 days).

### Brazilian-buyer specifics

- Brazilian Portuguese is mutually intelligible with European Portuguese but uses different vocabulary; the contract is in European Portuguese — Brazilian buyers should review with that in mind.
- Brazilian source-of-funds rules are well-established; lawyers familiar with Brazilian buyers know the documentation chain.

### Anglo-buyer specifics

- Most documents are in Portuguese. Lawyers will provide English translations of key sections, but the binding text is the Portuguese.
- Anglo buyers used to common-law systems can be surprised by the notary structure (no escrow agent in the U.S. sense; the notary is the mediator).
- "Closing costs" framing: budget ~7–10% of purchase price for IMT + IS + notary + registration + lawyer + (optional) survey, depending on bracket.

## Rental contracts (residential, long-term)

If the buyer is purchasing a tenanted property or planning to lease:

- Standard residential lease minimum term is 1 year, with renewal rules favoring tenants.
- Eviction rules favor tenants — significant friction to remove a non-paying tenant. Buyers paying for "vacant possession" should verify the property is genuinely vacant at escritura.
- Rent increases are regulated (annual indexation cap).
